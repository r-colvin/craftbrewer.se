Great question — you’re basically choosing between **“classic pub correct”** and **“homebrew-practical that still drinks right.”** For a 4 L nitro stout setup, you can absolutely do this well without pricey beer gas.

## TL;DR (my recommendation for your situation)
For **small volumes (4 L)** and **easy access to N₂ cartridges**:

👉 **Carbonate lightly with CO₂, then serve on pure N₂ through your stout faucet.**  
This is *exactly* how many homebrewers do convincing Guinness-style pours.

Beer gas is awesome, but it’s overkill (and expensive) at this scale.

---

## What Guinness is actually doing (important context)
Guinness isn’t magically nitrogenated beer. It’s:

- **Low-carbonated beer** (~1.2–1.4 vols CO₂)
- Served at **high pressure**
- Through a **restrictor (stout) faucet**
- With **nitrogen providing pressure**, not carbonation

Nitrogen’s job is:
- Push beer hard through the restrictor
- Create microbubbles
- Give that cascading pour + creamy head

CO₂ is still there — just not much of it.

---

## Option 1: Beer gas (70/30 N₂/CO₂)
### Pros
- Pub-authentic
- Maintains carbonation over time
- Set-and-forget serving

### Cons (for *you*)
- Expensive
- Overkill for 4 L
- Still requires higher pressures (25–35 PSI)
- Harder to justify unless you’re running a keg long-term

**Verdict:** Fantastic if you already have it. Not worth buying *just* for a 4 L stout.

---

## Option 2: CO₂ carb → serve with pure N₂ (your best fit)
### This works extremely well for small batches.

### How to do it
#### 1️⃣ Carbonate with CO₂ (lightly)
Target:
- **1.1–1.3 volumes CO₂**
- Roughly:
  - 8–10 PSI at 4 °C (39 °F), or  
  - Force carb briefly, then vent

You want it noticeably *less fizzy* than normal beer.

#### 2️⃣ Switch to N₂ for serving
- Disconnect CO₂ completely
- Hook up your **pure N₂**
- Serve at **30–40 PSI**
- Use your **stout faucet** (this is critical)

The restrictor + pressure + nitrogen does the magic.

---

## About using 2g N₂ cartridges
Totally viable — with caveats.

### What to expect
- **Pressure drops quickly** (tiny volume of gas)
- You may only get **1–2 good pours per cartridge**
- Best if:
  - Your system has **very little headspace**
  - Lines are short
  - Keg is cold

For a 4 L mini-keg, this is actually pretty reasonable.

💡 Tip: Chill the keg *fully* before serving — warm beer will eat gas fast.

---

## Pour technique (this matters a LOT)
Even perfect gas won’t save a bad pour.

1. Glass at 45°
2. **Full open** tap (never throttle)
3. Fill to ~¾
4. Let cascade settle (60–90 sec)
5. Top up straight down the middle

If it looks right but tastes flat → slightly more CO₂ next batch  
If it’s fizzy → too much CO₂, not a nitrogen problem

---

## Common mistakes to avoid
- ❌ Carbonating fully on CO₂ first (you’ll get foam soup)
- ❌ Trying to carbonate with nitrogen (it won’t dissolve)
- ❌ Low serving pressure on N₂ (kills cascade)
- ❌ Long narrow lines (adds unwanted restriction)

---

### 🤖 Assistant

Great questions — this is exactly where **pure N₂ + small kegs** behaves a little differently than pub systems, and a few habits will save you gas *and* beer quality.

I’ll break it into the two scenarios you asked about.

---

# 1️⃣ During the serving period (keg partly empty, larger headspace)

**Your main goals here:**
- Preserve the remaining CO₂ in the beer  
- Avoid wasting nitrogen  
- Prevent oxidation  
- Keep pours consistent after idle periods

### Best practice (short answer)
**After each session:**
1. **Turn off the nitrogen regulator**
2. Leave the keg sealed
3. Keep it cold
4. Do *not* vent

That’s it.

### Why this works
- Nitrogen is essentially insoluble → it won’t change the beer
- CO₂ will *slowly* come out of solution if headspace pressure drops
- Turning off the reg prevents slow leaks or pressure creep

When you come back days later:
- Reconnect N₂
- Bring back to **30–35 PSI**
- First pour may be a touch flat-looking, second pour will be perfect

💡 **Do not purge headspace with nitrogen** between sessions — you’ll strip CO₂ and burn cartridges.

---

## Headspace getting larger over 1–2 weeks
This is unavoidable, but manageable.

### What actually happens
- Headspace fills mostly with **nitrogen**
- CO₂ slowly equilibrates into the headspace
- Beer carbonation *very gradually* declines

Over **1–2 weeks at cold temps**, the loss is small — usually not perceptible for stout.

---

## One small improvement (optional)
If you want to be extra careful:

- After a pouring session, drop pressure to **~10–15 PSI**
- This slows CO₂ migration without wasting much N₂
- Next session, bring back to serving pressure

Not mandatory — just a refinement.

---

# 2️⃣ Storage before serving (keg full, small headspace)

This is the *easy* case.

### Ideal setup
- Kegged
- Purged with CO₂
- Sealed
- Cold

### Best practice
**Store on CO₂, not nitrogen**

#### Why
- CO₂ protects carbonation level
- Prevents oxygen ingress
- Keeps beer stable for weeks

### Practical workflow
1. Keg the beer
2. Purge headspace thoroughly with **CO₂**
3. Chill
4. Hold at **5–8 PSI** (or disconnected after purge)

You can leave it like this **indefinitely** (weeks to months).

---

## When you’re ready to serve
1. Chill fully (if not already)
2. Add/confirm light carbonation (~1.1–1.2 vols)
3. Vent headspace
4. Disconnect CO₂
5. Connect N₂ and serve

This transition costs very little gas and preserves beer quality.

---

# 3️⃣ What NOT to do (important)
❌ Store the keg long-term on nitrogen  
❌ Periodically “refresh” headspace with N₂  
❌ Vent headspace repeatedly during storage  
❌ Leave the regulator attached between sessions “just in case”

All of these either flatten the beer or waste cartridges.

---

# 4️⃣ Realistic expectations for your timeline
For **4 L over 1–2 weeks**, cold, sealed, and handled as above:

- Oxidation risk: **negligible**
- Carbonation loss: **minimal**
- Flavor change: **none you’ll notice**

This is actually a very forgiving style for this setup.

---

## Bottom-line cheat sheet
**Before serving (full keg):**  
→ CO₂ purge, store cold, low CO₂ pressure or disconnected

**During serving (partially empty):**  
→ Turn N₂ off between sessions, don’t vent, keep cold
