# Materials register

Homebrewing equipment is made from a surprisingly wide range of materials. A single fermenter setup may contain five or six distinct plastics, two or three elastomers, and one or more metals — each with its own chemical resistance profile, its own food contact regulatory history, and its own failure mode under the chemicals that get applied to it. This page catalogues all of them.

The register is organised by material family: polyolefins and commodity polymers first, then engineering thermoplastics, then elastomers, then metals and inorganic materials. Each entry covers what the material is, where it appears in homebrewing equipment, its food contact regulatory status, and its compatibility with the four chemical environments it is most likely to encounter: acid-based no-rinse sanitiser (ABNS), ethanol sanitiser (DES), alkaline cleaning agents (PBW and equivalents), and beer and wort.

The rating system uses five grades:

- **A** — compatible. No meaningful degradation under typical homebrewing use conditions.
- **B** — use with caution. Compatible in most conditions but with specific limitations around concentration, temperature, contact duration, or geometry.
- **C** — limited use. Marginal compatibility; acceptable only in specific, controlled circumstances.
- **D** — avoid. Significant degradation risk or meaningful toxicology concern.
- **X** — incompatible. Do not use.

Where a rating carries important qualifications — and many do — those qualifications are in the text, not collapsed into the grade. A B rating with five lines of caveats is a different thing from a B rating with one.

**Relationship to other pages:** The ratings in this register are grounded in the wet-dry cycle (WDC) model, which explains why ratings differ between brief sanitiser contact and a dried residue scenario. For the mechanism behind why some ratings are use-case-dependent, see [The wet-dry cycle model](./03-wdc-model.md). For health and toxicology implications, see [Toxicology](./04-toxicology.md). Equipment-specific guidance cross-references back to this page for per-component ratings.

## Polyolefins and commodity polymers

### Polypropylene (PP)

Polypropylene is the most common plastic in homebrewing equipment. Fermenter buckets, lids, taps, bottle washers, airlocks, and a range of accessories are made from PP. It is a semi-crystalline polyolefin with an all-carbon backbone and no functional groups susceptible to hydrolysis, making it chemically stable across a wide range of aqueous environments.

**Food contact status:** One of the most extensively approved food contact plastics in the world. Listed in EU Regulation 10/2011 (plastics food contact materials) with established specific migration limits for its monomer (propylene, SML 5 mg/kg) and common additives. The Plast-Box Declaration of Conformity for the Witre fermenter buckets demonstrates what documented PP compliance looks like: migration-tested against all five EU simulants by an accredited laboratory (J.S. Hamilton Poland, PCA No. AB 079), with all results well within the overall migration limit of 10 mg/dm² under Regulation 10/2011.[^1]

Temperature limit: the Plast-Box DoC specifies continuous use up to 45°C (maximum filling temperature 95°C — meaning a hot product can be placed into the bucket, but the bucket itself should not be held above 45°C). Do not clean PP with boiling water or run hot wort directly into a cold PP fermenter.

**Compatibility — ABNS:** A. PP has no functional groups susceptible to acid attack at pH 3–3.5. The ISM chemical compatibility chart rates PP as A (suitable/no effect) for phosphoric acid across the relevant concentration range. DDBSA at working dilution (~300 ppm) presents minimal risk — the non-polar PP matrix is not significantly penetrated by anionic surfactant at these concentrations. Under WDC conditions (DDBSA concentrated to ~75,000 ppm after drying), the situation is more aggressive: DDBSA begins to interact with the hydrocarbon matrix at high concentrations, particularly at stress points. However, for the open interior surface of a fermenter (Zone A geometry — no confinement, no mechanical stress), even repeated WDC cycles take well over 200 events before approaching a concern threshold, and post-batch cleaning with PBW resets accumulation between batches. In realistic homebrewing use, PP fermenter bodies are not a materials concern for ABNS.[^2]

The components where Zone B and Zone C geometry create elevated WDC risk — tap thread roots, lid clip recesses, bulkhead washers — are covered in the individual equipment guides. The PP fermenter body itself is at Zone A.

**Compatibility — DES:** A. Ethanol at 70–80% has minimal interaction with PP. The non-polar PP matrix is not swollen or penetrated by ethanol at these concentrations; ISM rates PP as A for all ethanol concentrations up to and including 100%.

**Compatibility — PBW:** A. Alkaline cleaning agents at the concentrations used in homebrewing (typically 0.5–2%) do not attack PP. PBW (sodium metasilicate/percarbonate base) is A-rated for PP.

**Compatibility — beer/wort:** A. PP is routinely used in commercial food contact applications with aqueous, acidic, and mildly alcoholic products. No concern.

**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** A | A | A | A

### High-density polyethylene (HDPE)

HDPE is used for fermenter taps (KegLand's preferred tap material), spray bottle bodies, storage containers, and chemical storage applications. It is a polyolefin like PP, but with a linear rather than branched backbone, giving it higher density, better chemical barrier properties, and better resistance to stress cracking than low-density variants.

**Food contact status:** A-rated globally. EU Regulation 10/2011 compliant. HDPE is used for milk jugs, detergent bottles, and chemical storage containers — its food and chemical contact safety profile is extensively documented.

**Compatibility — ABNS:** A. HDPE rates A for phosphoric acid and anionic surfactants at working dilution. The higher crystallinity of HDPE versus PP provides slightly better resistance to hydrocarbon and surfactant penetration.

**Compatibility — DES:** A at brief-to-moderate contact. A note: at 80% ethanol in sustained contact (weeks to months of storage), HDPE can show slow ethanol permeation and minor swelling. For a spray bottle that is refilled and emptied regularly, this is not a concern. For long-term DES storage, HDPE is acceptable but glass or specifically-rated alcohol storage containers are technically superior. For brew-day use, HDPE is A.

**Compatibility — PBW:** A.

**Compatibility — beer/wort:** A.

MDPE (medium-density polyethylene), sometimes found in older equipment, has similar compatibility to HDPE in all relevant brewing chemical environments.

**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** A | A | A | A

### Polyethylene terephthalate (PET)

PET is the vessel material for the FermZilla All Rounder (30L), the Oxebar 4L mini keg and 500 mL bottles, and the KegLand amber PET bottles. It is a semi-crystalline polyester identifiable by the ♻️1 recycling code. Its primary chemical vulnerability is the ester linkage in the backbone, which is susceptible to hydrolysis under sustained acid or base contact — but the rates at brewing-relevant conditions are measured in years, not days.

**Food contact status:** Among the most thoroughly regulated food contact materials in the world. EU Regulation 10/2011 lists PET with specific migration limits for its monomers: terephthalic acid (SML 7.5 mg/kg) and ethylene glycol (SML 30 mg/kg). PET is used globally at scale for carbonated beverages including beer, sparkling water, and cola, all of which are more aggressive contact environments than working-dilution ABNS. This is the most important contextual fact for PET compatibility: a PET bottle of cola contacts phosphoric acid at pH 2.5 for weeks on a warm retail shelf. Working-dilution ABNS at pH 3.0–3.5 for 30–60 seconds is a much less demanding scenario.[^3]

**Compatibility — ABNS:** A at brief contact. The phosphoric acid component at working dilution and ambient temperature presents no meaningful hydrolysis risk — the half-life for PET ester hydrolysis at pH 3–3.5 and 20°C is measured in years. The DDBSA component interacts weakly with the aromatic terephthalate rings in the PET backbone, but at working dilution concentrations, the effect is minor and reversible. Brief ABNS sanitisation contact is well within safe parameters for PET. Sustained immersion — leaving ABNS in a PET vessel for days — is not recommended for extended periods but is documented as safe by KegLand for the FermZilla All Rounder: StellarSan can be stored in the vessel indefinitely.[^4] Do not exceed 40°C when cleaning PET — the Tg of PET is approximately 70–80°C but hot water approaching 60°C causes increased plasticiser mobility and accelerated hydrolysis.

**Compatibility — DES:** A. Ethanol has minimal interaction with PET at 70–80%.

**Compatibility — PBW:** A at normal cleaning temperatures. Alkaline hydrolysis of PET ester linkages is genuinely more aggressive than acid hydrolysis, but at 0.5–2% PBW at 40°C and normal cleaning contact times (15–30 minutes), the ester hydrolysis rate is slow enough to be a non-concern in practice.

**Compatibility — beer/wort:** A.

**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** A | A | A | A

### Tritan copolyester

Tritan is an Eastman-developed copolyester used in the FermZilla Tri-Conical collection container. It is a BPA-free alternative to polycarbonate with similar optical clarity and impact resistance. Its comonomers include 2,2,4,4-tetramethyl-1,3-cyclobutanediol (TMCD), which provides greater hydrolytic stability than standard PET through increased steric hindrance around the ester linkage.

**Food contact status:** A. FDA-approved, EU Regulation 10/2011 compliant, confirmed free of oestrogenic activity by independent testing. Used in baby bottles and food storage applications.

**Compatibility — ABNS:** B. The ester linkage is more resistant than standard PET due to the TMCD structure, but brief ABNS contact carries modest hydrolytic risk over repeated cycles. Appropriate for the collection container role, where ABNS contact is brief and brew-day-limited. The heat resistance advantage over PET (Tritan handles contact up to 100°C, which PET cannot) makes it a materially better choice for a component that may receive hot water cleaning.

**Compatibility — DES / PBW / beer:** A across all three.

**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** B | A | A | A

### Polystyrene (PS) — GPPS and SAN grades

PS in homebrewing appears primarily as airlock bodies and, in some cases, bottle washer basins. Two grades are relevant: General-Purpose Polystyrene (GPPS) and Styrene-Acrylonitrile copolymer (SAN). These are chemically distinct and carry different ratings.

**GPPS** is amorphous, transparent, brittle, and has pendant phenyl groups that interact strongly with DDBSA via aromatic pi-pi stacking. DDBSA's aromatic benzene ring interacts with the PS phenyl groups, facilitating penetration into the polymer matrix. At working dilution, this produces surface stress-whitening and brittleness over repeated contact. At WDC-concentrated levels (~75,000 ppm), the interaction is severe. The result is rated B at working dilution, D under WDC conditions. GPPS is therefore a genuine concern if it appears in a component receiving repeated ABNS contact that is allowed to dry — such as the interior of an airlock.

**SAN** is a copolymer of styrene and acrylonitrile. The acrylonitrile component is polar and partially blocks the aromatic interactions that make GPPS susceptible to DDBSA. SAN rates A for ABNS contact — the polar character of the acrylonitrile groups is the difference.

The practical problem: unless a product specification explicitly states SAN, you cannot assume it from appearance alone. Both are transparent and visually similar. For airlocks, which contact ABNS fill liquid in the liquid trap, grade identification matters. If unconfirmed, treat as GPPS.

**Food contact status:** Both GPPS and SAN are EU Regulation 10/2011 compliant with migration limits for their monomers (styrene SML 0.045 mg/kg; acrylonitrile SML not detected). The styrene migration limit is stringent — this reflects regulatory concern about styrene as a potential carcinogen at high exposures — but the migration rate from compliant PS articles into cold aqueous contact is sufficiently low to be well within limits in normal use.

**Compatibility (GPPS) — ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** B | A | A | A  
**Compatibility (SAN) — ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** A | A | A | A

### Polycarbonate (PC)

PC was used in older fermenter vessels and carboys. It has been largely eliminated from current homebrewing equipment due to BPA migration concerns — polycarbonate releases bisphenol A (BPA) into contact media, particularly at elevated temperatures and under alkaline cleaning conditions. BPA is a recognised endocrine disruptor with an EU-harmonised specific migration limit of 0.05 mg/kg under Regulation 10/2011.

**Compatibility — ABNS / DES / PBW:** Chemically compatible in the sense that PC is not dissolved by these chemicals, but the BPA migration concern makes PBW contact at elevated temperatures a health risk. PBW alkalinity accelerates BPA migration from PC.

**Compatibility — beer/wort:** A if the vessel is undegraded and used at ambient temperature, but the migration concern under cleaning conditions accumulates over time.

**Practical position:** If you have PC equipment, replace it. The food contact standard has moved on and current equipment is better in every respect.

**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** A | A | A | A (if undegraded — but the toxicology concern overrides the compatibility rating)

## Engineering thermoplastics

### Polyketone (POK)

POK is the material used in current-generation KegLand DuoTight push-fit fittings (collars and bodies), the RAPT Pill hydrometer body, and other KegLand precision components. It is a relatively recent engineering thermoplastic produced by copolymerisation of CO and ethylene/propylene. KegLand's designation for their glass-reinforced grade is GR-POK.

POK's defining chemical resistance advantage over its predecessor POM is the nature of its carbonyl groups. In POM (acetal), the ether-linked oxymethylene backbone can undergo acid-catalysed chain-unzipping — a progressive depolymerisation producing formaldehyde. POK's carbonyl groups are not ether-type and do not participate in this mechanism. The result is stability across a wide range of acid concentrations, including phosphoric acid at any brewing-relevant concentration and temperature.

**Food contact status:** POK is EU 1935/2004 compliant for food contact applications. GR-POK (glass-reinforced) grade food contact status for KegLand's specific formulation is pending written confirmation from KegLand EU — this is noted as an open item.

**Compatibility — ABNS:** A. No susceptibility to the phosphoric acid or DDBSA components at working dilution or WDC-concentrated levels. This was specifically validated by KegLand's internal testing during the DuoTight design revision process.[^5]

**Compatibility — DES / PBW / beer:** A across all three.

**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** A | A | A | A

---

### Polyoxymethylene (POM / Acetal)

POM, also known as acetal, Delrin, or polyformaldehyde, is the original material used in DuoTight push-fit fitting collars and remains the material in John Guest push-fit fittings. It is a high-precision engineering thermoplastic with excellent mechanical properties but a specific and serious vulnerability to acid sanitisers under wet-dry cycle conditions.

The failure mechanism is acid-catalysed depolymerisation (chain-unzipping) at the oxymethylene backbone. Phosphoric acid — present in all ABNS sanitisers — acts as a catalyst for this reaction. At working dilution and brief contact, the rate is low. After WDC evaporation to CF=250, the phosphoric acid concentration in the dried residue approaches 2.4 M — sufficient to make chain-unzipping a real and progressive risk. The products of POM degradation include formaldehyde, which is a confirmed carcinogen (IARC Group 1) and has an EU SML of 15 mg/kg in food contact materials under Regulation 10/2011.[^6]

The DuoTight design revision document describes the failure mode explicitly: ABNS dried on POM collars under mechanical stress (the collar is compressed by the push-fit mechanism) caused environmental stress cracking, leading to sudden structural failure of the fitting. This is the most complete cause-and-effect materials failure chain documented in the homebrewing literature.[^5]

**Compatibility — ABNS:** D to X for repeated contact with drying. Brief contact without drying is lower concern, but there is no scenario in normal sanitising practice where you can guarantee no drying occurs. Do not use POM components in any surface that contacts ABNS sanitiser.

**Identifying POM in equipment:** POM is typically natural (white/off-white) or black, dimensionally precise, and has a distinctive sweet chemical odour when machined or cut. It has no standard consumer recycling code. If a push-fit fitting is not explicitly identified as POK and is not the current KegLand DuoTight product (post-revision), treat it as potentially POM until confirmed.

**Compatibility — DES / PBW / beer:** A. POM is not attacked by ethanol, alkaline cleaners, or beer at ambient temperature.

**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** D–X | A | A | A (if undegraded)

### Nylon 12 (Polyamide 12)

Nylon 12 appears in some equipment accessories and sealing components. It is a polyamide with a long, flexible aliphatic backbone that gives it low moisture absorption compared to shorter-chain nylons (Nylon 6, Nylon 6,6), making it better suited to aqueous environments.

**Food contact status:** EU 10/2011 compliant. The specific migration limits for its caprolactam content — a concern for shorter-chain nylons — do not apply to Nylon 12, which does not use caprolactam as a monomer.

**Compatibility — ABNS:** A. The amide linkages in Nylon 12 are resistant to hydrolysis at the pH of working-dilution ABNS (pH 3.0–3.5). Acid hydrolysis of polyamides requires stronger acid concentrations and elevated temperatures. Brief ABNS contact at ambient temperature is not a concern.

**Compatibility — DES / PBW / beer:** A across all three.

**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** A | A | A | A

## Elastomers

Elastomers are the components most likely to determine the compatibility of a piece of equipment with ABNS sanitisers. Small elastomeric components — grommets, tap washers, O-rings, valve seats — are often less carefully documented than the vessel they are attached to, are made from a wider range of materials than the plastics in the same assembly, and have more varied chemical resistance profiles.

The single most important principle for elastomers: the chemical resistance profile of EPDM is not the same as silicone, which is not the same as NR, which is not the same as Viton. Knowing that something is "rubber" tells you nothing useful.

### EPDM (Ethylene Propylene Diene Monomer rubber)

EPDM is the assumed material for the KegLand KL01625 fermenter airlock grommet — the standard black grommet supplied with Witre bucket fermenter kits. Confirmation from KegLand EU is an open item, but the black colour and hardness are consistent with EPDM rather than NR. The 29-page Rubber Group chemical compatibility chart, which covers benzenesulfonic acid (the closest available proxy for DDBSA) against a wide range of elastomers, is the primary reference for EPDM DDBSA ratings.[^7]

EPDM is a saturated backbone elastomer (M-class per ASTM D1418), which provides significantly better oxidative and thermal stability than unsaturated backbone rubbers like NR and SBR. It is the default food contact elastomer for many applications and is A-rated for most aqueous environments, including dilute acids and alkaline cleaners.

**Compatibility — ABNS:** The phosphoric acid component of ABNS at working dilution rates A for EPDM — the saturated backbone has no susceptibility to acid hydrolysis across any brewing-relevant concentration range.

The DDBSA component tells a different story. EPDM rates U (unsatisfactory) against benzenesulfonic acid in the Rubber Group chart. The mechanism: DDBSA's hydrophobic C12 alkyl chain partitions into the non-polar EPDM hydrocarbon matrix, causing swelling, plasticiser extraction, surface energy reduction, and progressive embrittlement. The U rating reflects sustained immersion at high concentration.

At working dilution (~300 ppm) and brief contact — the conditions of a fermenter sanitisation cycle — the practical risk is much lower than the U rating implies. The critical question is whether the WDC mechanism (see [the WDC model](./03-wdc-model.md)) concentrates DDBSA to aggressive levels at the grommet surface. For a fermenter airlock grommet, the WDC exposure is bounded: the grommet is wetted during the sanitisation event, fermentation dilutes any residue with condensation over days or weeks, and post-batch cleaning resets accumulation. The grommet sees at most one meaningful WDC cycle per brew, not an accumulating exposure. With normal brewing practice — PBW cleaning post-batch, ABNS at correct dilution — EPDM grommets are manageable. The rating for this bounded, realistic scenario is B.

The distinction between U (laboratory rating under sustained immersion) and B (realistic homebrewing scenario with post-batch cleaning) is not hand-waving — it is the whole point of the WDC model.

**Compatibility — DES:** A. EPDM is resistant to ethanol across the full range.

**Compatibility — PBW:** A.

**Compatibility — beer/wort:** A.

**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** U/B | A | A | A

(U/B: U under sustained immersion; B under realistic bounded contact with post-batch cleaning reset.)

### Silicone (platinum-cured)

Food-grade silicone is polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) — a synthetic polymer with a silicon-oxygen backbone rather than the carbon-carbon backbone of hydrocarbon rubbers. This structural difference gives silicone its distinctive combination of wide temperature range (−50°C to +200°C continuous), chemical inertness, and flexibility at low temperatures.

Two cure systems exist and the distinction matters for food contact applications. **Platinum-cured** silicone uses a platinum catalyst that is consumed in the curing reaction, leaving essentially no residue. **Peroxide-cured** silicone uses organic peroxides that decompose during curing but can leave residual peroxide decomposition products. For food contact applications, platinum-cured is the correct specification. KegLand's silicone bungs are explicitly confirmed as platinum-cured and food contact grade.

Silicone appears in homebrewing as: tap washers, airlock grommets (where a correct geometry can be sourced — see below), silicone transfer hose, and bungs. The FermZilla All Rounder pre-2023 lid O-ring was silicone (black); post-2023 units use the LOW2 elastomer (brown).

**Food contact status:** Silicone is not covered by a harmonised EU regulation in the way that PP and PET are. Compliance is typically declared against national standards — Germany's BfR Recommendation XV is the most commonly cited European standard for food contact silicone. A product referencing BfR XV compliance has a meaningful regulatory basis. FDA 21 CFR §177.2600 is the US equivalent. EU Regulation 1935/2004 (general food contact framework) applies regardless.

A regulatory flag worth noting: the cyclic siloxanes D4 (octamethylcyclotetrasiloxane) and D5 (decamethylcyclopentasiloxane) are listed as SVHC (Substances of Very High Concern) under REACH. Both are present at low levels in silicone food contact articles and migrate into contact media at detectable but low concentrations. The current regulatory position is that migration levels from compliant silicone articles are within acceptable limits, but the regulatory status of D4/D5 is evolving. This is a flag for monitoring rather than an immediate concern.

**Compatibility — ABNS:** B. The Si-O backbone has no susceptibility to acid hydrolysis and silicone is inert to phosphoric acid. For DDBSA, silicone rates better than EPDM — ISM charts rate silicone as B (minor effect) versus EPDM's U for benzenesulfonic acid equivalents. The non-polar hydrocarbon portion of DDBSA still interacts with the PDMS matrix to some extent, causing minor swelling that is largely reversible after contact. At WDC-concentrated levels, this interaction increases but remains below the threshold of structural concern for short contact durations. Silicone is the best available elastomer for tap washers and grommets in ABNS contact — its B rating against DDBSA is meaningfully better than EPDM's U.

An outstanding sourcing note: no confirmed food-grade silicone flat grommet in the correct geometry for standard bucket fermenter lids has been identified. Until one is confirmed and stocked by a Swedish or EU supplier, EPDM remains the practical grommet material for this application, with silicone as the stated preference if sourcing is resolved.

**Compatibility — DES:** A at normal sanitiser contact. At 70–80% ethanol in prolonged immersion (silicone bung used as an ethanol-fill airlock fluid), silicone shows moderate swelling — rated B–C for sustained contact. For the spray-and-drain sanitisation use case, A.

**Compatibility — PBW:** A.

**Compatibility — beer/wort:** A. Silicone is suitable for sour beer contact — the Si-O backbone is more acid-stable than EPDM at low pH for extended durations.

**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** B | A | A | A

### Natural rubber (NR)

Natural rubber is the latex of *Hevea brasiliensis*, vulcanised with sulphur-based cure systems. It remains in circulation in homebrewing equipment because it is cheap, elastic, and widely available. It should not be.

NR has a polyisoprene backbone with alternating double bonds — a structure that is susceptible to oxidation, ozone attack, acid-catalysed degradation, and swelling in organic solvents including high-concentration ethanol. Its typical cure system includes thiuram and dithiocarbamate accelerators that can release N-nitrosamine precursors and their degradation products into contact media. N-nitrosamines are classified by the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) as probable or confirmed human carcinogens (Group 2A/Group 1 depending on the specific compound).[^8]

The toxicology concern for NR is the decisive issue, not its chemical compatibility. A grommet or washer that is intact, correctly installed, and not visibly degraded still has the same N-nitrosamine precursor leaching potential. This makes NR unique among homebrewing materials: it is the only one where visible inspection of the component does not provide meaningful reassurance about safety.

**Compatibility — ABNS:** D. NR rates D to U for benzenesulfonic acid at working dilution — swelling, softening, and accelerated degradation. At WDC-concentrated levels (CF=250), the rating approaches X. Do not use NR components in contact with ABNS.

**Compatibility — DES:** C–D. NR has poor ethanol resistance at 70–80% concentration. Swelling and extraction of vulcanisation accelerators occur.

**Compatibility — beer/wort:** D. N-nitrosamine precursor migration into contact media is the primary concern. Sour beer contact at low pH with long conditioning times (weeks to months) increases the migration rate.

**Practical position:** Replace any NR grommets, washers, and O-rings immediately. Use EPDM as a minimum replacement. This is the highest-priority action in the entire materials register — not because the risk is the largest in absolute terms, but because it is invisible to inspection and entirely preventable. There is no scenario in homebrewing where NR is the correct choice over available alternatives.

**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** D | C–D | — | D

### Styrene-butadiene rubber (SBR)

SBR is a synthetic rubber used as an inexpensive alternative to NR. Like NR, it has an unsaturated backbone (in this case, from the butadiene component), making it susceptible to oxidative degradation and chemical attack. Like NR, it is typically sulphur-cured with accelerators that produce N-nitrosamine precursor compounds.

SBR has no advantages over NR for homebrewing applications and shares all of its risks. The assessment is identical: replace immediately, use EPDM as a minimum alternative.

**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** D | C–D | — | D

---

### Nitrile rubber (NBR)

NBR is a copolymer of butadiene and acrylonitrile. It is not common in fermenter components but appears in some bottling wand seals (described as "food grade" on KegLand bottling wand product pages). Its acrylonitrile (ACN) content — typically 18–50% by composition — determines its chemical resistance profile: higher ACN gives better oil and chemical resistance but reduced cold flexibility.

Compared to NR and SBR, NBR has substantially better chemical resistance due to the polar nitrile groups, which block some of the partitioning interactions that damage NR in DDBSA contact. Compared to EPDM and silicone, it is worse.

**Compatibility — ABNS:** C. NBR rates B for dilute phosphoric acid (the nitrile groups provide better acid resistance than NR) but C for DDBSA at working dilution — moderate swelling through hydrophobic partitioning. At CF=250, the rating approaches D. Brief contact during bottling wand sanitisation is manageable; sustained soaking is not recommended.

**Compatibility — DES:** C. NBR has limited ethanol resistance at 70–80% concentration. Swelling occurs, though less severely than NR.

**Compatibility — PBW / beer:** A for both.

**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** C | C | A | A

### Viton (FKM fluoroelastomer)

Viton is DuPont's brand name for fluoroelastomers — polymers with a partially or fully fluorinated backbone that provides exceptional resistance to a wide range of aggressive chemicals. Viton appears in homebrewing as spray bottle seals (Biltema pH 1–9 bottle), O-rings, and some tap seals.

**A critical distinction:** Viton comes in multiple cure systems, and they have meaningfully different chemical resistance profiles at low pH. **Peroxide-cured (PP-grade) Viton** is resistant to phosphoric acid across a wide concentration range and rates A–B for ABNS contact. **Polyamine-cured (PA-grade) Viton** is rated Not Recommended for phosphoric acid in the Epoca spray bottle compatibility chart — a manufacturer source identifying cure-system-specific incompatibility with the acid component of ABNS.[^9]

This distinction is not commonly stated on product pages or in equipment specifications. A component identified as "Viton" without cure system confirmation leaves a significant gap. Until the cure system is confirmed by the manufacturer, Viton cannot be reliably assigned a grade A for ABNS — the rating depends entirely on which type it is.

**Compatibility — ABNS (peroxide-cured):** A–B. Resistant to phosphoric acid and DDBSA at working dilution. Some minor swelling in DDBSA at high concentrations; manageable in realistic contact durations.

**Compatibility — ABNS (polyamine-cured):** C. Not Recommended for phosphoric acid — the polyamine cure system is attacked by the phosphoric acid component of ABNS, causing progressive degradation.

**Compatibility — DES:** B for both grades. Viton can swell in high-concentration ethanol — a documented limitation despite its general reputation for broad chemical resistance.

**Compatibility — PBW / beer:** A for both grades.

**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer (peroxide):** A–B | B | A | A  
**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer (polyamine):** C | B | A | A

### Santoprene (TPV — Thermoplastic Vulcanisate)

Santoprene is an EPDM/PP thermoplastic vulcanisate — a rubber-toughened thermoplastic that combines the chemical resistance profile of EPDM with the processing advantages of thermoplastics. It appears in homebrewing as the KegLand Oxebar Gen2 elbow (confirmed by KegLand) and the NukaTap valve shuttle.

Its chemical resistance follows EPDM for aqueous environments — good for phosphoric acid, manageable (bounded) for DDBSA at working dilution, excellent for alkaline cleaners and beer.

**Compatibility — ABNS:** A–B. Following EPDM logic: A for phosphoric acid; bounded-B for DDBSA under realistic contact conditions with post-batch cleaning.

**Compatibility — DES / PBW / beer:** A across all three.

**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** A–B | A | A | A

### LOW2

LOW2 is KegLand's proprietary elastomer specification used for lid O-rings in the FermZilla All Rounder (post-2023, brown O-ring), the 5L and 9L mini kegs, and the Cornelius keg lid O-ring. The yellow colour and ASTM D1418 M-class designation ("Synthetic M-Class and Cross-Linked Elastomers" per KegLand's product description) are most consistent with a specialised, high-crosslink-density EPDM formulation, though KegLand has not published the base polymer specification.

The defining performance characteristic is oxygen transmission: KegLand states LOW2 achieves 27 times lower oxygen transfer than standard silicone or nitrile.[^10] This was independently tested by GunnLab, an Australian packaging testing laboratory, and the test report is available on the product page. A 27× reduction in oxygen permeation through the lid O-ring translates directly to extended shelf life for oxygen-sensitive beers.

**Compatibility — ABNS:** B. If the base polymer is EPDM-derived M-class, the same bounded analysis applies as for standard EPDM: adequate for brief wet contact during CO₂-purge sanitisation; the lid O-ring's groove geometry limits sustained immersion; one WDC cycle per sanitisation event, reset by post-batch cleaning. EU food contact compliance for LOW2 specifically is an open item pending written confirmation from KegLand EU.

**Compatibility — DES / PBW / beer:** A across all three (following EPDM chemistry).

**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** B | A | A | A

### EVABarrier (EVA/EVOH)

EVABarrier is KegLand's brand name for their beer line tubing — a multilayer construction with an ethylene vinyl alcohol (EVOH) barrier core layer bonded to ethylene vinyl acetate (EVA) inner and outer layers. EVOH provides very low gas permeability (oxygen and CO₂), while EVA provides flexibility and chemical resistance.

**Food contact status:** EVA and EVOH are both EU 10/2011 listed materials. EVOH is also used extensively in commercial food packaging barrier films.

**Compatibility — ABNS / DES / PBW / beer:** A across all four chemical environments. EVA is resistant to aqueous acids, dilute alkalis, ethanol, and alcoholic beverages. EVOH layers enhance rather than degrade this resistance. No concerns in any homebrewing chemical environment.

**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** A | A | A | A

## Metals

### Stainless steel 304 and 316L

Stainless steel is the reference construction material for commercial brewing equipment and appears in homebrewing as Cornelius kegs, mini kegs, NukaTap bodies, and pressure-rated vessels. Both 304 and 316L (the molybdenum-containing marine grade) are used; the difference matters primarily for chloride-containing cleaning agents (316L has better pitting corrosion resistance) and in sustained high-chloride contact environments. For homebrewing with standard cleaning and sanitising chemistry, 304 is entirely adequate.

The corrosion resistance of stainless steel depends on the passive oxide layer (primarily chromium oxide, Cr₂O₃) that forms spontaneously on the surface. This layer is thermodynamically stable at the pH ranges of all brewing chemicals and is self-healing if damaged. Chloride ions are the principal threat — they can penetrate the oxide layer and initiate pitting corrosion. ABNS sanitisers contain no chloride; Star San, Sanipro Rinse, StellarSan, and Chemsan are phosphoric acid / DDBSA based. PBW does not contain chloride. Chloride risk comes from: using bleach or chlorinated tap sanitisers without thorough rinsing, and in extreme cases from chlorinated municipal water. Normal homebrewing practice with the chemicals discussed in this guide poses no pitting corrosion risk to stainless steel.

Passivation (immersion in dilute citric or nitric acid to regenerate the oxide layer) is recommended for new stainless equipment and after mechanical damage to the surface, but is not required as a routine step in normal homebrewing cleaning cycles.

**Compatibility — ABNS / DES / PBW / beer:** A across all four environments. Stainless is one of the least chemically constrained materials in the register.

**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** A | A | A | A

---

### Glass

Glass (borosilicate and soda-lime) is chemically inert to all brewing chemicals at ambient temperature and normal concentrations. It is the reference material against which food contact safety is tested (EU simulant A is distilled water; simulant B is acetic acid — both simulate conditions in glass). Glass airlocks, test jars, and fermenters present no compatibility concerns.

**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** A | A | A | A

### PTFE (polytetrafluoroethylene)

PTFE is chemically inert to essentially all reagents at homebrewing-relevant temperatures and concentrations. It appears as thread seal tape, filter discs, and some gaskets. No compatibility concerns exist. PTFE is the most chemically inert polymer in common use and is the reference material for chemical resistance comparisons.

**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** A | A | A | A

### Aluminium

Aluminium does not appear in the main fermentation or packaging equipment chain covered by this guide. It can appear as: airlock foil covers for flask openings during starter preparation; foil used as a sterile dust cover; and in some older or artisanal equipment.

Aluminium's corrosion resistance depends on a thin (2–5 nm) aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃) passivation layer. Phosphoric acid at working dilution (0.01M) dissolves this layer at a rate of approximately 0.1–0.5 nm/minute. In 30–60 seconds of contact, the dissolution amounts to approximately 0.05–0.5 nm of the 2–5 nm oxide layer — leaving a substantial remaining oxide layer and releasing approximately 1 microgram of aluminium from a 10 cm² surface into 1 litre of liquid (1 ppb). The WHO guideline for aluminium in drinking water is 200 µg/L — a safety margin of approximately 200× for brief working-dilution contact.[^11]

At WDC conditions after drying (phosphoric acid concentrated to approximately 2.4M), the situation changes substantially. 2.4M phosphoric acid is genuinely aggressive toward aluminium oxide. The manufacturer warnings against using ABNS sanitisers on aluminium equipment are valid in this specific scenario — it is not the working dilution contact that is the concern, it is the concentrated dried residue.

**Compatibility — ABNS:** B for brief wet contact; D for WDC exposure (do not apply ABNS to aluminium and allow to dry).

**Compatibility — DES / beer:** A.

**Compatibility — PBW:** A at pH and temperatures used in homebrewing.

**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** B (wet) / D (dry) | A | A | A

## Specialised components

### Crown cap liners (oxygen-scavenging)

Oxygen-scavenging crown cap liners contain an iron-based chemistry that reacts irreversibly with oxygen. This reaction is activated by moisture — specifically, by the liquid present in the headspace of a bottle after filling. The mechanism is: moisture activates the iron compound, which then scavenges oxygen from the headspace gas.

The critical implication for sanitiser contact: if the liner is wetted with water or sanitiser before use, the oxygen-scavenging chemistry is partially or fully consumed before the cap is applied to the bottle. The cap may seal correctly but have no remaining oxygen-scavenging capacity. Brewers sometimes sanitise all caps in a batch at the start of a bottling session, then apply them over the course of an hour or more — by the time later caps are applied, the chemistry may already be depleted.

**Compatibility — ABNS / DES:** X (functional deactivation). The cap material itself is not degraded, but the oxygen-scavenging function is destroyed.

**Correct procedure:** Do not pre-sanitise oxygen-scavenging crown caps. Sanitise bottles; apply caps directly from their packaging without wetting. The bottle's interior has already been sanitised; the cap needs only to be clean (manufacturer cleanliness from packaging is sufficient) not sanitised with aqueous chemicals.

**ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer:** X | X | — | A

## Notes on identification

Several of the most important distinctions in this register — NR versus EPDM grommets, GPPS versus SAN airlocks, peroxide-cured versus polyamine-cured Viton, POM versus POK push-fit collars — cannot be determined by visual inspection alone. Materials that are different in their compatibility and toxicology profiles can be visually identical.

When equipment documentation does not specify the material of elastomeric or plastic components to this level of detail, the safest approach is: ask the manufacturer directly (a documented response from the manufacturer is a primary source), cross-reference the product against a known compliant specification, or choose a different product where the material is documented. In the specific case of NR — where the toxicology risk is invisible to inspection — there is no acceptable alternative to confirmation or replacement.

The [Documentation problem](./06-documentation-problem.md) page covers this gap in detail: what equipment sellers are required to disclose, what they typically do disclose, and how to read the gap between them.
