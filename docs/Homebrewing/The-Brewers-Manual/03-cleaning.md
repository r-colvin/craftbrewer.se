# Cleaning

*This page follows the EU regulatory framework and Swedish market context described in the [introduction](./01-introduction.md).*

Cleaning is not the same thing as sanitising, and the distinction matters in both directions. Cleaning removes organic soil — yeast residue, protein deposits, hop resins, beer stone. Sanitising reduces microbial load on a *clean* surface to levels that cannot cause infection. Sanitiser applied over organic contamination cannot reliably reach the microbial cells embedded beneath the soil layer. The sequence is non-negotiable: **clean first, sanitise second**.

The good news is that most infections in homebrewing trace back to inadequate cleaning rather than inadequate sanitising. A thoroughly cleaned surface that receives adequate ABNS contact time is reliably safe. A sanitised surface that was not first cleaned properly is not.

## The Sinner Circle

The Sinner Circle — named for Herbert Sinner, who described it in the context of industrial cleaning in 1959 — is a useful mental model for all cleaning decisions. It identifies four factors that together determine cleaning effectiveness:

**Chemistry** — the cleaning agent and its mechanism of action. Alkaline cleaners hydrolyse proteins and saponify fats. Oxidising cleaners break apart organic soil through electron transfer. Mechanical action is not chemistry, but the cleaning solution must be chemically appropriate for the soil type.

**Temperature** — elevated temperature accelerates nearly every chemical cleaning mechanism. The exception in homebrewing is when temperature is constrained by vessel material — PET, in particular, limits the cleaning temperature you can safely use.

**Mechanical action** — agitation, flow, turbulence, or direct scrubbing. Mechanical action supplements chemistry; it removes loosened soil and brings fresh cleaning solution into contact with the surface. Shaking a vessel, brushing threads, and using a bottle brush are all mechanical action.

**Time** — contact time determines how completely the chemistry and temperature can act on the soil. Longer contact time can compensate for reduced chemistry, temperature, or mechanical action — up to a point.

Reducing any one factor requires increasing one or more of the others to achieve the same result. This trade-off is not arbitrary — it means there is no single correct cleaning protocol, only protocols appropriate to the equipment, soil level, and constraints in play.

In homebrewing, temperature is often the binding constraint (PET vessels cannot be cleaned at the hot temperatures that work best for alkaline cleaners). The response is extended soak time with adequate chemistry at the highest temperature the vessel allows.

## Cleaning vs. "just rinsing"

Before addressing specific products, the most important practical recommendation in this entire section:

**The single highest-impact cleaning action is an immediate cold water rinse immediately after emptying any vessel, before organic soil has had time to dry.**

Dried yeast cake, dried beer stone, and dried hop resin all require substantially more chemistry, temperature, and time to remove than fresh, wet residue. Rinsing immediately after use — while residue is still liquid — reduces cleaning effort for every subsequent step by an order of magnitude.

This is not cleaning. A cold water rinse removes wet residue mechanically but does not kill microorganisms, does not remove dried deposits, and is not a substitute for a cleaning product. But as a preparation step, it is the most efficient use of 30 seconds in the brewing process. Empty the fermenter; add two litres of cold water; shake; drain. Empty the keg; fill with cold water; push through the posts; drain. Do it immediately. Clean properly later.

Water alone — without a cleaning agent — is appropriate only for this immediate post-use rinse. It is not adequate as the sole cleaning method for taps, narrow-geometry fittings, kegs, or any equipment with dried residue.

## Alkaline cleaners — StellarClean and PBW

The dominant cleaning chemistry for homebrewing is alkaline percarbonate. Two products in this class are in common use: **StellarClean** (KegLand) and **PBW** (Five Star Chemicals, US). They share the same core mechanism but are meaningfully different formulations.

### What they have in common

Both products contain sodium percarbonate (2Na₂CO₃·3H₂O₂) as the primary active ingredient. Dissolved in water, sodium percarbonate releases hydrogen peroxide and sodium carbonate. The hydrogen peroxide provides oxidative action — breaking apart protein bonds, denaturing enzymes, attacking biofilm matrix. The sodium carbonate provides alkalinity (pH 10–11 in solution) that saponifies lipids and accelerates protein hydrolysis. This mechanism is well-matched to the soil profile of brewing equipment: yeast cells are protein and lipid; beer stone (calcium oxalate) responds to alkalinity; hop resins are partly saponifiable.

Both products require rinsing after use — the alkaline residue would interfere with ABNS sanitiser effectiveness if left on surfaces.

### How they differ

The critical difference is the alkaline booster: PBW uses **sodium metasilicate** (~30% of the formulation); StellarClean uses **sodium silicate** (25%) and adds a substantial ionic surfactant package (40% of the formulation).[^1][^2]

**Sodium metasilicate** (PBW) is more strongly alkaline than sodium silicate — PBW solution at 1% concentration reaches approximately pH 12.[^2] This makes PBW more aggressively effective on heavy protein and hop resin soils, particularly at elevated temperature. PBW is designed for hot use: 54–82°C for CIP, 60°C for hot soak.[^2]

**Sodium silicate** (StellarClean) is less strongly alkaline. The lower pH and the buffering effect of the surfactant package make StellarClean less aggressive toward polymer and PC surfaces. KegLand explicitly states that StellarClean is safe on polycarbonate; PBW does not make this claim, because its higher alkalinity is more hydrolytic of the polycarbonate ester linkage.

**Ionic surfactants** (StellarClean, 40% by weight) are the key addition. Surfactants reduce surface tension, allowing the cleaning solution to penetrate soils and wet all contact surfaces — including the hydrophobic surfaces of hop resins and lipids. They surround and suspend removed soil particles to prevent redeposition. KegLand notes that repeated use of percarbonate alone (without surfactants) allows build-up of oils and hydrophobic residues that percarbonate cannot address.[^1] The surfactant component handles these. StellarClean's surfactant concentration also means it remains effective at lower temperatures, which PBW's metasilicate component does not.

### Which to use

| Equipment | Recommended cleaner | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Stainless kegs (Cornelius, mini kegs) | Either — PBW hot soak preferred for heavy soiling | No temperature or PC concern with stainless |
| PET fermenter (FermZilla, Oxebar) | **StellarClean** | Lower alkalinity; effective cold; PBW requires strict time and temperature limits on PET |
| PP bucket fermenter | Either | No temperature concern with PP at practical cleaning temperatures |
| Equipment with PC components | **StellarClean** | Explicitly PC-safe; PBW higher risk at pH 12 |
| Heavy hop resin or persistent yeast | PBW hot soak (stainless only) | Metasilicate is more aggressive on stubborn organics |
| Cold soak, overnight | StellarClean | Effective at all temperatures; PBW metasilicate is slower cold |

### PET temperature and time limits

PET begins to show measurable surface changes under working-concentration alkaline cleaner at approximately 30–40°C after extended contact. The practical limit for PBW on PET is **cold or warm water (maximum ~40°C), maximum 30 minutes**. In practice, 15–20 minutes with agitation is sufficient for most beer residue and leaves margin before the limit is approached.

StellarClean's lower alkalinity is more forgiving on PET and is effective cold, making it the more practical choice for PET vessels where temperature discipline is a constraint.

The EVOH barrier layer in the Oxebar 4L adds complexity — cleaning that damages the PET surface layers could compromise barrier layer bonding. The same cold/warm temperature and 30-minute time discipline applies; StellarClean is the appropriate choice.

Stainless kegs have no temperature or time constraint from PBW — hot PBW at 60°C for 30–60 minutes is the standard effective commercial protocol and the stainless is entirely unaffected.

## Oxidising cleaners — StellarOxy and equivalents

Oxidising cleaners such as StellarOxy (KegLand) use sodium percarbonate or peracetic acid at concentrations designed to address biofilm, persistent organic staining, and hop resin build-up that alkaline cleaners alone do not fully address.

StellarOxy uses strong oxidative action to break apart the organic matrix of biofilm and to bleach pigmented deposits (beer stone discolouration, hop resin staining). It is appropriate as a supplemental cleaner for equipment with persistent soiling or for routine use in serving systems where biofilm prevention is important.

Oxidising cleaners generally require more careful handling of exposure time and temperature on polymer equipment — check the product's specific instructions for each material type.

## Dishwasher tablets — an honest assessment

Dishwasher tablets are widely used in homebrew cleaning, and the chemistry is worth understanding properly. At their core, most dishwasher tablets contain sodium carbonate and/or sodium percarbonate (the same alkaline base as PBW and StellarClean), with the addition of proteases and amylases (enzymes that break down protein and starch), TAED (a bleach activator for the percarbonate component), and in many formulations, a rinse aid component.

The enzymatic content is a genuine advantage for brewing residue — protease and amylase action on yeast cells and protein deposits is directly relevant. Neither PBW nor StellarClean contains enzymes.

The practical comparison:

| Property | StellarClean / PBW | Dishwasher tablet (chlorine-free) |
|---|---|---|
| Core chemistry | Alkaline percarbonate | Alkaline percarbonate |
| Enzymatic action | No | Yes — advantage for brewing residue |
| Chlorine risk | No | Check formulation — variable |
| Fragrance / rinse aid risk | No | Check formulation — variable |
| PET time limit | 30 minutes | 30 minutes (similar pH) |
| Stainless compatibility | Excellent | Excellent (chlorine-free) |
| Formulation certainty | High — known composition | Lower — varies by brand |

The limitation is formulation certainty. Dishwasher tablets vary significantly between brands, and some contain chlorine compounds (hypochlorite bleach activators) that are aggressive toward stainless steel and toward some elastomers. Always use **chlorine-free** dishwasher tablets for brewing equipment. Similarly, fragranced tablets can leave residues that affect beer flavour.

For standard cleaning of ale fermenters and kegs from unhopped or lightly hopped beers, chlorine-free dishwasher tablets are an adequate and economical alternative to dedicated brewing cleaners. For heavily hopped beers with persistent resin build-up, PBW's metasilicate alkalinity may outperform dishwasher tablets — and the formulation certainty of a dedicated brewing cleaner is genuinely valuable.

Rinse thoroughly after using any dishwasher tablet: the rinse aid component leaves a slightly alkaline residue that can interfere with ABNS effectiveness if not neutralised.

## Temperature constraints by material

Cleaning temperature constraints derive from material limits, not chemistry preferences. The relevant limits for common equipment:

| Material | Maximum cleaning temperature | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| PP bucket fermenter | ~80°C | Far above any practical cleaning requirement; no thermal constraint |
| HDPE tap | ~80°C | No practical constraint |
| PET (FermZilla, Oxebar) | ~40°C | Binding constraint; use cold or slightly warm water only |
| EPDM grommet | ~100°C | Rated for steam; no brewing cleaning constraint |
| Stainless 304/316 | No practical limit | Hot PBW standard; no concern |
| Glass | No practical limit | Caution with thermal shock on cold glass |
| Silicone tubing | ~200°C | Can be boiled; most resistant material for heat cleaning |

Do not use hot water or steam on PET equipment. Do not use abrasive cleaners — scourers, abrasive powders, or rough sponges — on any plastic equipment. Abrasion scratches the surface, creating crevices that trap soil and microorganisms, and increases microplastic particle release from the surface. A soft cloth, soft sponge, or purpose-made bottle brush with soft bristles is appropriate for all plastic brewing equipment.

## Cleaning sequence — post-brew

The recommended post-brew cleaning sequence for a bucket fermenter and Oxebar setup:

1. **Immediate rinse:** as soon as the fermenter is emptied — before doing anything else — rinse with cold water, shake, drain. Repeat for any vessels that held beer.

2. **Disassemble the tap:** unscrew the tap from the bucket, remove the nozzle if applicable. The tap thread root (Zone B geometry) accumulates concentrated ABNS residue and yeast/protein soil in the thread recess. This geometry cannot be cleaned effectively without disassembly. Brush the threads with a small bottle brush or pipe cleaner. Soak in cleaning solution.

3. **Prepare cleaning solution:** StellarClean at working dilution in warm water (not hot for PET; any temperature for PP or stainless). Soak all components for 15–30 minutes. Agitate where possible.

4. **Rinse:** thorough water rinse until the rinse water runs clear and pH-neutral. Alkaline cleaner residue on surfaces will reduce ABNS effectiveness at next sanitisation if not fully removed.

5. **Inspect:** while equipment is wet and rinsed, check seals, grommets, and tap washers. EPDM is black and may discolour but should remain supple. A hardened, cracked, or white-powdered seal needs replacement. The tap washer at the thread junction is the most frequently degraded component.

6. **Dry and store clean:** store clean equipment dry. Storing damp, uncleaned equipment creates conditions for mould and biofilm development before the next brew.
