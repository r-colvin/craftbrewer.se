# Polycarbonate (PC)

| | ABNS | DES | Cleaning | Beer/wort |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Rating** | A | A | A | A† |

*† Rating reflects chemical compatibility only. The toxicology concern — BPA migration — overrides the compatibility rating. See below.*

PC was the dominant material for transparent carboys and is still found in siphons, bottling wands, sight glasses, and some airlocks — BrewTools airlocks, for example, use PC. It has been largely eliminated from fermenter vessels due to BPA (bisphenol A) migration concerns, but it remains in circulation in accessories and process equipment. BPA is a recognised endocrine disruptor with an EU-harmonised specific migration limit of 0.05 mg/kg under [Regulation 10/2011](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:02011R0010-20230923).

## Food contact status

PC is [EU Regulation 10/2011](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:02011R0010-20230923) compliant with a specific migration limit for BPA of 0.05 mg/kg. Compliance requires that actual BPA migration from the article remains below this limit under tested conditions. However, BPA migration from PC accelerates at elevated temperatures and under alkaline cleaning conditions — PBW contact at temperatures above 40 °C is a particular concern. Accumulated cleaning cycles can cause PC articles to exceed BPA migration limits over time.

## Compatibility — ABNS / DES / Cleaning: A (chemical compatibility only)

PC is not dissolved or structurally degraded by ABNS, DES, or PBW at homebrewing concentrations. The chemical compatibility rating is **A**.

The toxicology concern is separate: PBW alkalinity accelerates BPA migration from PC, meaning chemically compatible cleaning progressively increases the BPA exposure risk from the article. The material is chemically stable; the migration product is the concern.

## Compatibility — beer/wort: A† (if undegraded)

No compatibility concern for beer contact. The BPA migration concern is the operative issue.

## Practical position

For fermenter vessels: replace with PET, PP, or Tritan. These are better in every material respect, BPA-free by design, and have better-documented food contact compliance.

For accessories and process equipment where PC remains the current material — siphons, bottling wands, sight glasses, BrewTools airlocks — the picture is more nuanced. The BPA migration concern is real but contact-time-dependent: transit contact (wort flowing through a siphon, beer flowing past a sight glass) is a much lower migration scenario than sustained immersion in hot alkaline cleaning solution. The highest-risk practice with PC accessories is aggressive alkaline cleaning at elevated temperature. If you use PC accessories, clean them at room temperature with a mild alkaline cleaner, avoid prolonged hot-alkaline soak cycles, and replace any articles showing surface crazing or cloudiness.

For the toxicology context, see [Toxicology and migration](../06-toxicology.md). For the WDC model implications for any PC equipment still in use, see [The wet-dry cycle model](../04-wdc-model.md) — the same accumulation mechanism applies.
