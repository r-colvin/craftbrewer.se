# Polystyrene — GPPS and SAN

These two materials are addressed together because the entire point of the discussion is the difference between them. They look identical. They have meaningfully different chemical resistance. Knowing which grade you have determines the rating.

## GPPS — General-Purpose Polystyrene

| | ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer/wort |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Rating** | *B* (working dilution) / *D* (WDC) | *A* | *A* | *A* |

GPPS is amorphous, transparent, and brittle. It has pendant phenyl groups that interact strongly with DDBSA via aromatic π–π stacking: DDBSA's aromatic benzene ring interacts with the PS phenyl groups, facilitating penetration into the polymer matrix.

At working dilution, this produces surface stress-whitening and progressive embrittlement over repeated contact — rated *B*. At WDC-concentrated levels (~75,000 ppm DDBSA after full drying), the interaction is severe — rated *D*.

GPPS is therefore a genuine concern if it appears in a component receiving repeated ABNS contact that is allowed to dry, such as the interior of an airlock that is filled with ABNS solution.

## SAN — Styrene-Acrylonitrile copolymer

| | ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer/wort |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Rating** | *A* | *A* | *A* | *A* |

SAN is a copolymer of styrene and acrylonitrile. The acrylonitrile component is polar and partially blocks the aromatic interactions that make GPPS susceptible to DDBSA. SAN rates *A* for ABNS contact — the polar character of the acrylonitrile groups is the chemically significant difference.

## Food contact status

Both GPPS and SAN are [EU Regulation 10/2011](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:02011R0010-20230923) compliant with migration limits for their monomers (styrene SML 0.045 mg/kg; acrylonitrile SML not detectable). The styrene migration limit is stringent — this reflects regulatory concern about styrene as a potential carcinogen at high exposures — but the migration rate from compliant PS articles into cold aqueous contact is sufficiently low to be well within limits in normal use.

## The identification problem

Unless a product specification explicitly states SAN, you cannot assume it from appearance alone. Both grades are transparent and visually similar. For airlocks, which contact liquid in the liquid trap, grade identification matters.

**If the grade is unconfirmed, treat as GPPS.** The open item for Enolandia airlocks (GPPS vs. SAN grade confirmation) is recorded in the [documentation problem](../02-documentation-problem.md) page.
