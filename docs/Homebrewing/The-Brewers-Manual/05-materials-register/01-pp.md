# Polypropylene (PP)

| | ABNS | DES | PBW | Beer/wort |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Rating** | *A* | *A* | *A* | *A* |

Polypropylene is the most common plastic in homebrewing equipment. Fermenter buckets, lids, taps, bottle washers, airlocks, and a range of accessories are made from PP. It is a semi-crystalline polyolefin with an all-carbon backbone and no functional groups susceptible to hydrolysis, making it chemically stable across a wide range of aqueous environments.

## Food contact status

One of the most extensively approved food contact plastics in the world. Listed in [EU Regulation 10/2011](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:02011R0010-20230923) (plastics food contact materials) with an established specific migration limit for its monomer (propylene, SML 5 mg/kg) and common additives.

The Plast-Box Declaration of Conformity for the Witre fermenter buckets demonstrates what documented PP compliance looks like: migration-tested against all five EU simulants by an accredited laboratory (J.S. Hamilton Poland, PCA No. AB 079), with all results well within the overall migration limit of 10 mg/dm² under Regulation 10/2011.[^1]

**Temperature limit:** The Plast-Box DoC specifies continuous use up to 45 °C (maximum filling temperature 95 °C — meaning a hot product can be placed into the bucket, but the bucket itself should not be held above 45 °C). Do not clean PP with boiling water or run hot wort directly into a cold PP fermenter.

## Compatibility — ABNS: *A*

PP has no functional groups susceptible to acid attack at the pH of working-dilution ABNS (pH 3–3.5). The ISM chemical compatibility chart rates PP as *A* (suitable/no effect) for phosphoric acid across the relevant concentration range.[^2] DDBSA at working dilution (~300 ppm) presents minimal risk — the non-polar PP matrix is not significantly penetrated by anionic surfactant at these concentrations.

Under WDC conditions — DDBSA concentrated to approximately 200,000 ppm (20% by mass) in the dry residue at CF≈667 for StellarSan at the manufacturer's recommended dilution — the interaction is more aggressive: DDBSA begins to interact with the hydrocarbon matrix at high concentrations, particularly at stress points.[^2] However, for the open interior surface of a fermenter (Zone A geometry — no confinement, no mechanical stress), the per-cycle residue quantity is very small (3.7 mg spread across 2,550 cm² is a 15 nm film), and rinsing or cleaning between batches resets accumulation. The full analysis of how many WDC cycles Zone A PP can accumulate before approaching a concern threshold will be in the materials register accumulation charts when written. In realistic homebrewing use, PP fermenter bodies are not a materials concern for ABNS.

:::note WDC accumulation charts
Per-material accumulation charts — mapping WDC exposure against damage thresholds by zone — are a planned addition to this register.
:::

The components where Zone B and Zone C geometry create elevated WDC risk — tap thread roots, lid clip recesses, bulkhead washers — are covered in the individual [equipment guides](../Equipment/index.md). The PP fermenter body itself is at Zone A.

## Compatibility — DES: *A*

Ethanol at 70–80% has minimal interaction with PP. The non-polar PP matrix is not swollen or penetrated by ethanol at these concentrations; ISM rates PP as *A* for all ethanol concentrations up to and including 100%.

## Compatibility — PBW: *A*

Alkaline cleaning agents at the concentrations used in homebrewing (typically 0.5–2%) do not attack PP. PBW (sodium metasilicate/percarbonate base) is *A*-rated for PP.

## Compatibility — beer/wort: *A*

PP is routinely used in commercial food contact applications with aqueous, acidic, and mildly alcoholic products. No concern.
